3D reconstruction is the process of capturing the physical shape and appearance of a real-world object, scene, or structure and converting it into a digital three-dimensional model. This is typically achieved by compiling numerous overlapping photographs or video frames and using specialized algorithms to calculate depth, geometry, and texture. The result is a highly accurate digital replica that can be viewed, rotated, and analyzed from any angle. As computing power has advanced, this technology has moved beyond specialized laboratories and into the hands of researchers, historians, and even citizen archivists, making it an increasingly accessible tool for documentation.

By creating permanent digital surrogates, we effectively insulate our collective memory from physical destruction. These models allow scholars to conduct detailed examinations without touching fragile originals, enable virtual tourism for those unable to travel, and provide crucial reference data for future restoration efforts. In short, 3D reconstruction transforms ephemeral, vulnerable heritage into enduring, shareable digital assets that can outlast the passage of centuries.
